Problem 1¶

Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are independent normal random variables with the same mean $\mu$ and the same variance $\sigma^2$. Do the random variables $W = X + Y$ and $U=2X$ have the same distribution? Explain.

No, the distributions will have the same mean but the variances will be different. W will have $Mean = E[X] + E[Y] = \mu + \mu = 2\mu$ and U will have $Mean = E[2X] = 2 * E[X] = 2 * \mu = 2\mu$. The variance of W will simply be $Var(W) = Var(X) + Var(Y) = \sigma^2 + \sigma^2 = 2\sigma^2$, but the variance of U will be $Var(W) = Var(2X) = 4 * Var(X) = 4 * \sigma^2 = 4\sigma^2$.

Problem 2: Central Limit Theorem and Simulation¶

a) For this problem, we will be sampling from the Uniform distribution with bounds [0, 100]. Before we simulate anything, let's make sure we understand what values to expect. If $X \sim U(0, 100)$, what is $E[X]$ and $Var(X)$?

For a random variable $X$ ~ $Unif(0, 100)$:
$$E[X] = \frac{b - a}{2} = \frac{100 - 0}{2} = 50$$
$$Var(X) = \frac{(b - a)^2}{12} = \frac{(100 - 0)^2}{12} = \frac{2500}{3}$$

b) In real life, if we want to estimate the mean of a population, we have to draw a sample from that population and compute the sample mean. The important questions we have to ask are things like:

  • Is the sample mean a good approximation of the population mean?
  • How large does my sample need to be in order for the sample mean to well-approximate the population mean?

Complete the following function to sample $n$ rows from the $U(0, 100)$ distribution and return the sample mean. Start with a sample size of 10 and draw a sample mean from your function. Is the estimated mean a good approximation for the population mean we computed above? What if you increase the sample size?

uniform.sample.mean = function(n){
    
    #set.seed(123)
    sample.mean <- mean(runif(n, min=0, max=100))
    return(sample.mean)
}

uniform.sample.mean(10)
uniform.sample.mean(1000)
uniform.sample.mean(500000)
51.9195414055139
50.4836439696839
50.0047665043357

The $n=10$ estimated mean is a poor approximation for the population mean because the standard deviation and variance are much too high with only 10 samples. For repeated trials of 10 samples we would expect to have estimated means greater than 60 and less than 40 frequently. Increasing the sample size will drive down the variance on the sample mean and give a more accurate result compared to the true population mean because sample size is inversely proportional to sample mean standard deviation. As the Law of Large Numbers predicts, our sample mean estimate of the true population mean approaches the theoretical value of 50 as we increase the number of trials.

c) Notice, for a sample size of $n$, our function is returning an estimator of the form

$$ \bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i $$

That means, if each $X_i$ is a random variable, then our sample mean is also a random variable with its own distribution. We call this disribution the sample distribution. Let's take a look at what this distribution looks like.

Using the uniform.sample.mean function, simulate $m=1000$ sample means, each from a sample of size $n=10$. Create a histogram of these sample means. Then increase the value of $n$ and plot the histogram of those sample means. What do you notice about the distribution of $\bar{X}$? Increasing the sample size from n=10 to n=1000 drastically reduced the spread of the distribution of the sample means. The range of the n=10 histogram appears to be approximately 20 to 80, while the range on the n=1000 histogram looks like 46 to 53, giving visual evidence that the spread of the sample means is much smaller with a higher number of samples per run, i.e. the variance is lower the more trials we perform. For $n=10$, the sample variance is $Var(\bar{X}) = \frac{(100-0)^2}{12} * \frac{1}{10} = \frac{250}{3}$ and for $n=1000$ it's $Var(\bar{X}) = \frac{(100-0)^2}{12} * \frac{1}{1000} = \frac{5}{6}$. The mean of $\bar{X}$ is always just the $E[X] = 50$ calculated in part a, and the mean is unaffected by the number of trials. Increasing trials only affects the spread, not the mean. Likewise, $\bar{X}$ is going to be normally distributed regardless of the selected value of n because of the Central Limit Theorem.

Problem 3¶

Let $X$ be a random variable for the face value of a fair $d$-sided die after a single roll. $X$ follows a discrete uniform distribution of the form $\textrm{unif}\{1,d\}$. Below is the mean and variance of $\textrm{unif}\{1,d\}$.

$$ E[X] = \dfrac{1+d}{2} \qquad Var(X) = \dfrac{(d- 1 + 1)^2 - 1}{12}$$

a) Let $\bar{X}_n$ be the random variable for the mean of $n$ die rolls. Based on the Central Limit Theorem, what distribution does $\bar{X}_n$ follow when $d=6$.

$\bar{X}_n$ would follow a normal distribution if n was sufficiently large enough to apply the CLT. The expectation value would be $E[\bar{X_n}] = \frac{1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6}{6} = 3.5$ and the variance would be $Var(\bar{X}) = \frac{(6 - 1 + 1)^2 - 1}{12} = \frac{35}{12}$, or $\bar{X}$ ~ $N(3.5, \frac{35}{12})$ and thus $\bar{X_n}$ ~ $N(3.5, \frac{35}{12n})$.

Finally, plot your running average per the number of iterations. What do you notice?
